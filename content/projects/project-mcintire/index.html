[Current Project] Characterizing natural flow regime of Hawaiian forest watersheds and its implication on downstream water availability

current project hydrology forest

PI: Dr. Yinphan Tsang

This project proposes to strategically characterize the natural flow regime of forest watersheds, the water resources protected by Hawai’i natural forests. This project proposes to support and train students and research assistants to perform various hydrological analyses to understand and reconstruct the natural flow regime of forested watersheds. The objectives of this study include:

  1. Examine types of forest watersheds and their typical streamflow response to precipitation, and describe their natural flow regime
  2. When data available, perform hydrologic analyses and/or modeling to address or simulate stream flow dynamics of the forest watersheds

This project will gather preliminary data and ultimately applying to large federal agency proposals. The potential future study topics include but not limit to: a.) evapotranspiration of native and invasive plants and the relation to cloud formation and stream flow in Hawai’i, and b.) the dynamics of flow, nutrient and sediment transport downstream that support own stream wildlife, cropland, and coastal ecosystem. The goal of this project is to establish a baseline of natural flow regime from healthy forest watersheds. Ultimately, we would like to evaluate the possibility to describe water availability upon future climate.
