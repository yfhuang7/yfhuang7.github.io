[Finished Project] Shifting magnitude and seasonality of streamflow extremes and the relationship with rainfall across the Hawaiian Islands

finished project hydrology climate seasonality

(This is the first topic of my Ph.D. dissertation.)

Current status:

Manuscript has been published in 2021: Huang, Y.F., Tsang, Y.P., Strauch, A. M, and Clilverd, H. M. 2021 “Shifting magnitude and seasonality of streamflow extremes and the relationship with rainfall across the Hawaiian Islands”

Abstract

Flooding is a significant threat to life and property in Hawaiʻi. As climate warming continues to alter precipitation patterns and hydrological processes in tropics, characterizing the shifting patterns in magnitude, seasonality, and location of floods will improve our understanding of the consequences of changing climate and better prepare us for future flooding events. In this study, 84 rain gauges and 111 crest gauges across five major Hawaiian Islands were analyzed from 1970 to 2005. We estimated trends in annual maximum daily rainfall (RFmax) and annual peak flow (PFmax) using the Mann-Kendall test and Senʻs slope. Subsequently, we examined the association between both trends and assessed temporal shifting by combining circular analysis with Senʻs slope. The main identified trends were a decrease in RFmax and PFmax (67% and 61% of the gauges, respectively) between 1970 and 2005. However, PFmax increased significantly at two gauges. The physiography (i.e. windward vs. leeward) has little contribution to both trends. In addition, the trend of RFmax cannot fully describe the trend of the PFmax since RFmax and PFmax did not occur coincidently. El Niño conditions result in earlier occurrence of PFmax and RFmax in the wet season. Besides, the trends of the RFmax and PFmax occurrence time shifted earlier in the wet season from 1970 to 2005, possibly because of the changes in El Niño. These findings have implications for assessing flood risk, that will aid watershed management and flood mitigation that can increase resilience of downstream communities and near-shore environments.

Highlight

  1. Both magnitude and occurrence time in annual maximum rainfall and annual peak flow shifted in Hawaiʻi.
  2. Annual peak flow did not coincide with annual maximum rainfall.
  3. The ENSO impacts on the occurrence time of annual maximum rainfall and peak flow in Hawaiʻi.
